Rank 12 M1 Abrams

Historical Background

The M1 Abrams (Tank, Combat, Full-Tracked: 105-mm Gun, M1) is an American main battle tank developed in the 1970s to replace the aging M60 Patton. After the US Congress canceled the MBT-70 and XM803 projects in late 1971, the Army resumed its M60 successor program, and by January 1973, the Pentagon presented its requirements for the tank. The Pentagon's requirements called for a tank gun with a caliber between 105 and 120 mm and a 20 to 30 mm Bushmaster cannon. The plans envisioned a tank weighing around 54 tons. In June, the Army awarded competitive three-year contracts to Chrysler and General Motors to produce prototypes. In February 1976, the two prototypes were tested at the Aberdeen Proving Ground. Chrysler chose an engine with a recuperative turbine manufactured by Avco Lycoming, while General Motors opted for a Teledyne Continental diesel engine. Both tanks were armed with a licensed version of the 105-mm Royal Ordnance L7 gun. In July, the Army recommended selecting the General Motors proposal, but the Pentagon ignored this recommendation, asking the competitors to modify their proposals, and in November, the Army selected the Chrysler design. This proposal was more attractive because the company stated it could incorporate a 120-mm Rheinmetall gun without increasing costs, weight, or production time. Between 1979 and 1985, 3,273 M1 Abrams tanks were produced, including the IPM1 modification. The tank was adopted by the US Army one year after production began in 1980. Over its service life, the tank has undergone numerous changes and modernizations, allowing it to remain in service today in many parts of the world.

Brief Overview

Maneuverability

Pros: Excellent mobility, the signature feature of the tank. The AGT-1500 gas turbine engine (1500 hp) provides monstrous acceleration, a high top speed (~79 km/h), and an excellent reverse speed (~42 km/h). The power-to-weight ratio (~27 hp/t) allows for quick positioning and flank changes.

Cons: The high weight (~56 t) leads to significant inertia when turning and bogging down on soft terrain. Driving at top speed requires careful handling.

Armor and Survivability

Pros: The first American tank with serious composite armor.

Turret: The frontal turret armor (especially its central part) is very strong at its optimal combat distance (1-1.5 km). It reliably withstands early 105-mm shells.

Survivability: A spacious fighting compartment with spaced crew members (4 people), isolated fuel tanks, and ammunition stowed in the turret rear bustle (behind blast doors) provide a high chance of surviving the first hit.

Cons: The upper front plate (UFP) of the hull is the main weakness. It can be penetrated by most APFSDS shells at its rank. The sides and rear are absolutely vulnerable. The gun mantlet ("cheeks") is another weak spot.

Armament

105-mm M68A1 rifled gun. Two-plane stabilizer. High accuracy and rate of fire (5 seconds with a fully upgraded crew).

Shells:

M774 (APFSDS): The primary combat shell. Allows you to effectively fight against the frontal armor of peer vehicles when aiming precisely at weak spots.

M735 (APFSDS): Base shell, weak for its rank. Should only be used while grinding the tank's modifications.

M393A2 (HESH): Effective against lightly armored vehicles.

M456A2 (HEAT-FS): Effective against medium and lightly armored vehicles.

Additionally: 2 x 7.62-mm and 1 x 12.7-mm machine guns.

Features

Good gun depression and elevation angles (-10°/+20°). Fast fire extinguishing system. Equipped with smoke grenades (12 pcs).

Conclusions and Comments

The M1 Abrams is a benchmark tank for support and tactical maneuvering. It combines good turret protection, phenomenal mobility, and an accurate gun, but it requires the player to maximize the use of terrain and tactical wit, as it is not impenetrable.
