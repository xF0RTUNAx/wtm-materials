Rank 13 Leopard 2 PL

Historical Background

The Leopard 2PL (in some Polish sources — Leopard 2PL/M1) is a modification of the German Leopard 2A4 main battle tank for the Polish Armed Forces. In 2002, Poland purchased 142 Leopard 2A4 tanks from Germany, for which a modernization plan to meet modern standards began to be developed in 2012. In December 2012, the modernization contract was included in the "Plan for the Technical Modernization of the Polish Armed Forces for 2013–2022". After lengthy negotiations in 2015 between the Polish companies Polska Grupa Zbrojeniowa (PGZ) and ZM Bumar-Łabędy SA, and the German Rheinmetall Landsysteme GmbH, a contract was signed for the modernization of 142 Leopard 2 tanks. Rheinmetall acted as a strategic partner, providing electronics, armament, and other components. Rheinmetall received €220 million (or 3.29 billion zł) for the work under this contract, and the vehicle itself was demonstrated to the public at the IDEX 2017 exhibition. The Leopard 2PL modernization program includes: the installation of additional 3rd+ generation modular armor on the hull and turret, an Auxiliary Power Unit (APU), upgraded commander's and gunner's sights with television channels, and due to the modernization of the Rh120 L/44 gun, DM11 and DM63 shells were added to the ammunition load. In September 2020, the 1st Warsaw Armored Brigade received the first two vehicles. By the end of 2023, 62 Leopard 2PL tanks had entered service with the Polish Army.

Brief Overview

Maneuverability

Pros: Good maneuverability metrics in all aspects, as befits NATO tanks. A benchmark for horizontal and vertical targeting speeds. It has the ability to keep up with other tanks and provides ample room for choosing tactics. Wide tracks, as well as a powerful engine, allow it to feel comfortable on various types of terrain.

Cons: There may be less responsiveness in handling during sharp maneuvers.

Armor and Survivability

In practice - a lack of hull armor. Accidental or lucky bounces/ricochets are possible. The greatest risks arise when taking damage to the sides. The turret can withstand the fire of many shells, especially ATGMs and APFSDS. The gun mantlet is also considered a weak spot. Additionally, composite screens are installed around the turret, protecting the vulnerable gun mantlet and the turret itself from kinetic shells. Such armor has variable thickness, meaning in some places the protection can reach 600 mm or more. The weak spots haven't gone anywhere, though: these are the aforementioned gun mantlet and the gunner's sight, hitting which will kill two crew members at once, and sometimes can even detonate the ammo rack.

Additionally: Do not take more than 16 shells for better survivability.

Armament

The gun features excellent ballistics, good armor penetration, and high accuracy.

Keep in mind that in terms of reload speed, we only outpace the Soviet autoloader. It is also demanding on crew upgrades to achieve the best reload time.

DM43 — APFSDS. A shell with a flight speed of up to 1660 m/s. The shell has excellent armor penetration and good post-penetration damage. Recommended as the primary shell for use.

The tank is armed with two machine guns. The first is coaxial with the main 120-mm gun. The second is an anti-aircraft machine gun mounted near the commander's cupola. They are only suitable for destroying obstacles.

Features

Blowout panels. Dual fire control system. Inability to shoot backward due to a design feature.

Conclusions and Comments

Recommended primarily for experienced players. A technical tank whose potential is unlocked considering knowledge of the theoretical base, the presence of gaming experience, and the ability to utilize its specific advantages while neutralizing permanent disadvantages. When playing, you should rely on the turret armor, but it's better to hide the hull due to its weak protection.
