4. General Conclusions

  1. The reason missile caliber is indicated is most likely related to the damage area of these missiles. Thus, depending on the missile's trajectory and the attacked ship's modules, the actual damage correlated with caliber has key practical significance in battle. (i.e., a "Moskit" will deal massive damage hitting ships with numerous unprotected modules in its attack path, and conversely - damage will be insignificant if there aren't enough vulnerable modules in the Moskit's path). The infographic provided in-game is for informational purposes based on trends for comparison and evaluation.
  2. Older missiles, especially smaller caliber ones, are balanced against more modern variants by reload speed. Thus, the practical damage of missiles and impact on module destruction remains unchanged. The damage range of some missiles is based on reload (i.e., potential, not actual damage equivalent per unit of time for comparison with analogs).
  3. There is no ultimate approach to choosing an effective fleet representative based solely on missiles. Different scenarios are possible in battles, not to mention that overall effectiveness is influenced by other specs and features. The combat trend at the time of publication is based on the dynamism of battles with extensive use of countermeasures. It can be concluded that the greatest versatility comes from fast reload (preferably with large caliber missiles) and reduced targeting time with adaptable launchers.
  4. Don't forget the importance of missile trajectories. As stated earlier, this affects practical damage. Despite using the same missiles, different fleet representatives have their own adaptations due to their launchers. More details about trajectories can be found in reviews of specific ships.

5. FXPENT (Fleet Evaluation)

FXPENT (FortX Practice Engine Navy Test) is an author's subjective technology for a quick and visual assessment of the effectiveness and versatility of fleet representatives.

Rating is based on: 1 - poor, 5 - excellent.

Maneuverability includes generalized impressions regarding linear speed, acceleration, and turn rate.

Survivability includes generalized impressions regarding countermeasures, direct raw survivability, and other indirect measures.

Missile armament includes generalized impressions regarding the missiles themselves, reload, quantity, trajectory, speed, and other indicators.

Auxiliary armament includes generalized impressions regarding torpedoes, mines, guns, and other indirect measures.

6. About Certain Missiles (AShM)

  1. P-1 "Strela"
    The most powerful missiles. They fly slowly. Countermeasures should be deployed in advance with a small time margin. Under ideal conditions, effective from about 1-1.5 km (straight trajectory), from the broadside about 2-2.5 km.
  2. Harpoon
    Not a high single-shot damage - compensates with quantity and reload speed. Quite slow with a long trajectory (often attack from above, especially at short distances from submarines). On newer fleet representatives, launchers are optimized (improved trajectory) alongside minimum reload time. Countermeasures can be used at any time, except at critical timer values (e.g., 5-6 sec or 1 sec). Often effective from about 2.5-3 km.
  3. MM38 Exocet
    Missiles with decent damage. Fly slowly and straight. Due to their launchers, they independently adapt their trajectory relative to the target. In ideal conditions from about 1.5-2 km, more often from about 2-2.5 km. It is optimal to deploy countermeasures with a slight delay due to the missile's speed.
  4. 85RU
    Fast missiles with good damage. The launcher always adapts to the optimal trajectory. Effective distance from about 2.5 km. Resembles a ballistic trajectory, especially at long distances, allowing it to overcome some obstacles. It's important not to miss the countermeasure deployment window, i.e., not too late, usually no later than 3 seconds, due to speed and trajectory.
  5. "Moskit"
    Fast and powerful missiles. Picky about broadside launches. Direct attacks without deviation are recommended in most scenarios. At minimum distances, launching from the stern is recommended. For Lun - from about 2.5 km, for Pr. 956 - from about 5 km. It is recommended to independently understand the philosophy of using countermeasures against these missiles based on practical battles due to the variety of trajectories and carriers, but mostly they have to be deployed with a time margin.
  6. "Termit"
    Slow but powerful missiles. Very long regular trajectory. No restrictions during regular launches, resembles launches from the stern of the Lun or Pr. 956. Picky about broadside launches, distance not less than about 3 km. From the stern from about 2.5 km. Countermeasures should be deployed with a slight delay.
  7. "Oniks"
    Quite fast and powerful missiles. Optimal trajectory for all launches, resembling a parabola. From about 2-2.5 km. Recommended to use decoys with a good but adequate delay at medium and long distances.
  8. "Kh-35"
    Roughly average speed, powerful missiles. Optimal trajectory when launched from the bow, but effective implementation is also possible when launching straight from the stern towards the enemy. From about 2-2.5 km. Picky about broadside launches. Recommended to use decoys 2-3 seconds before impact.
  9. ASW Missiles
    In practice, these are similar missiles but carry a torpedo as the payload and are only applicable against submarines. You don't need to overthink it and can just launch them all in one salvo. They often have launchers with horizontal guidance, simplifying effective implementation. Acoustic decoys are the only effective countermeasures against ASW missiles, best used slightly in advance. Sonar improves the effectiveness of ASW missiles.
