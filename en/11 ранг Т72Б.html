11 rank T-72B

Historical Background

The T-72B (Object 184) is a deep modernization of the T-72A tank. By the mid-1980s, there was a clear need to upgrade the Soviet Union's main battle tank, the T-72, to modern combat standards. The fact was that the vehicle severely lacked firepower and a modern fire control system, while its protection, which was decent at the time of its creation, began to become outdated due to the emergence of new ammunition.

The extensive tank modernization program involved a comprehensive improvement of the T-72 across all key characteristics. As part of this, the tank received an upgraded 125-mm 2A46M gun, an improved fire control system, and a guided weapon system. The frontal armor of the T-72 was also reinforced, supplemented by "Kontakt-1" explosive reactive armor (ERA) blocks. To maintain the previous mobility and dynamic performance, a more powerful engine was installed.

In December 1984, this tank, designated as the T-72B, was officially adopted into service by the USSR. It was this modification of the T-72 that became the basis for a large number of diverse versions and upgrades of the tank that are currently in service in many countries around the world. Between 1974 and 1990, Uralvagonzavod alone produced 20,544 T-72 tanks of various modifications, with about 10,000 more vehicles built in Warsaw Pact countries.

Brief Overview

Maneuverability

The T-72B tank possesses good cross-country mobility for its class and an acceptable maximum speed (about 60 km/h), ensuring confident task execution thanks to its high engine power-to-weight ratio. The main disadvantages are an extremely slow reverse speed (up to 4-5 km/h), limited maneuverability when turning on the spot, and poor ergonomics that complicate the crew's work.

Pros:

  • Good cross-country ability: The tank feels confident off-road, overcoming obstacles thanks to its wide tracks.
  • Dynamics: Acceptable acceleration and cross-country capability allow it to quickly take up positions.
  • Forward speed: The maximum highway speed is about 60 km/h.
  • Power-to-weight ratio: The tank maneuvers quite briskly, despite its solid weight.

Cons:

  • "Terrible" reverse speed: The main problem is the very low reverse speed (estimated by some at only 4-5 km/h), which makes a quick retreat from under fire difficult.
  • Turning on the spot: The lack of a proper turn-on-the-spot capability (neutral steering) makes the tank less agile in tight spaces or urban environments.
  • Ergonomics and visibility: The cramped fighting compartment and limited visibility can affect the crew's decision-making speed.
  • Transmission: The outdated transmission does not provide high maneuverability during sharp maneuvers.

Armor and Survivability

The T-72B (model 1985/1989) is a Soviet MBT with reinforced armor and high frontal survivability due to composite armor (NERA) and "Kontakt-5" explosive reactive armor (ERA). The front of the turret and hull are protected from most kinetic and shaped-charge threats of its time (up to 800-900 mm against HEAT). However, its survivability is low due to the dense layout and frequent ammo rack detonations.

Armament

The armament of the T-72B tank (model 1984/1985) includes a 125-mm 2A46M smoothbore gun/launcher, a coaxial 7.62-mm PKT machine gun, and a 12.7-mm NSVT anti-aircraft machine gun. The main feature is the 9K120 "Svir" guided weapon system (GLATGM) with laser beam riding missiles, as well as a fire control system (FCS) with a 1K13-49 sight.

Main armament elements:

  • Gun: 125-mm 2A46M-1 (or 2A46M) smoothbore gun, 48 calibers long, equipped with a 2E42-2 stabilizer.
  • Guided weapons: "Svir" system (9M119 missile), allowing ATGM fire at a distance of up to 4-5 km with a high probability of hitting the target, including helicopters.
  • Ammunition: 45 rounds of separate-loading ammunition (22 in the autoloader). Includes armor-piercing fin-stabilized discarding sabot (APFSDS), high-explosive anti-tank (HEAT), and high-explosive fragmentation (HEF) shells.

Conclusions and Comments

The T-72B requires gameplay based on frontal armor and defense, as mobility and targeting speed are limited. The main tactic is using the "aquarium" ("Kontakt-5" ERA) to withstand hits, positional play at medium/long distances, and firing with a 7-second reload. Avoid close combat and flanking maneuvers.

The tank feels best in positional warfare. Look for positions where the hull is hidden and play "hull-down" (from the turret). This is a "tanking" tank, not a fast assault vehicle. Support your allies, keep your distance, and use the terrain.

The tank has a slow gun targeting speed and poor mobility, making maneuvering combat difficult. Avoid close distances where you can be easily flanked and shot in the side/rear.

The autoloader provides a stable reload of 7 seconds. This is faster than many opponents, but slower than the T-80 (6 seconds). Use this advantage to suppress the enemy.
